Wave Height vs Beach Change

Correlation between maximum wave height during survey periods and mean elevation change demonstrates primary wave control on coastal morphodynamics.

Statistical Significance: Pearson correlation r = 0.61 (p < 0.001) indicates strong negative relationship between wave height and beach elevation. Higher waves consistently drive erosion, while lower wave periods allow beach recovery through accretion.

Analytical Methodology

Data Collection

Beach topography surveyed using DJI Phantom 4 RTK UAV at regular intervals from March 2018 to May 2025. Structure-from-Motion photogrammetry processed in Agisoft Metashape to generate high-resolution Digital Elevation Models (DEMs).

Analysis Framework

  • Mean Elevation Change (MEC): Average elevation difference between consecutive surveys across all measurement points
  • Beachface Cluster Dynamics (BCD): Jenks natural breaks classification of morphological states (erosional/neutral/accretional)
  • Residual BCD (r-BCD): Cumulative behavioral index characterizing overall regime of each transect
  • Cross-shore Zonation: Division into offshore, mid-beach, and onshore zones based on elevation profiles
  • Wave Correlation: Integration with offshore wave buoy data (Hsig, Tp, Dp) for forcing-response analysis

Publication

Journal Article

Title: Cross-shore vulnerability gradients and rock armour impacts on sandy beach evolution

Authors: Siegmund Nuyts, et al.

Journal: Coastal Engineering (In Review)

Study Period: 2018-2025 (54 drone surveys)

Location: Port Fairy Bay, Victoria, Australia (38°22'S, 142°14'E)

Abstract

This study investigates coastal vulnerability patterns and the influence of rock armour structures on sandy beach morphodynamics using seven years of UAV-derived topographic data integrated with offshore wave measurements from Port Fairy Bay, Australia. Analysis of 54 drone surveys reveals strong correlations between wave forcing and beach elevation change, with maximum wave height (r = 0.61, p < 0.001) showing significant control on erosion/accretion patterns. The research demonstrates elevation-dependent vulnerability gradients across the beach profile and identifies persistent behavioral regimes associated with coastal protection structures. Results indicate that rock armour creates localized erosional zones with 67% behavioral persistence, compared to 23% in natural beach sections. The methodology combines Mean Elevation Change (MEC) analysis with Beachface Cluster Dynamics (BCD) classification using Jenks natural breaks to characterize morphological states and transitions. Wave direction analysis reveals orientation-dependent responses, with transects aligned perpendicular to dominant swell directions experiencing greatest variability. Cross-shore zonation analysis shows distinct responses in offshore, mid-beach, and onshore zones, with intertidal regions demonstrating highest sensitivity to wave forcing. This work contributes to understanding climate change impacts on sandy coastlines and provides evidence-based insights for coastal management and adaptation strategies in embayed systems globally.

Research Impact

This research provides quantitative evidence for coastal management decision-making, demonstrating the complex interactions between natural processes and engineering interventions. The integrated monitoring approach and analytical framework are scalable to other Victorian Coastal Monitoring Program (VCMP) sites, enabling statewide coastal vulnerability assessment.
