Coastal Change Analytics for Victoria

CoastWatch-VIC is a comprehensive data platform designed to make Victorian coastal monitoring data accessible, interpretable, and actionable for coastal managers, researchers, and communities. By integrating high-resolution UAV surveys, wave monitoring, and advanced spatial analytics, we provide evidence-based insights into how Victoria's dynamic coastlines are changing over time.

Victoria's 2,500 km coastline faces unprecedented challenges from climate-driven sea level rise, increasing storm intensity, and anthropogenic interventions. Understanding these changes requires long-term, systematic monitoring and sophisticated analysis. CoastWatch-VIC transforms complex coastal datasets into clear visualizations and quantitative metrics that support informed coastal management decisions.

Our platform showcases cutting-edge research from sites across Victoria, demonstrating how innovative monitoring techniques and data analysis can reveal elevation-specific vulnerabilities, identify wave thresholds for morphological change, and quantify the impacts of coastal protection structures. Each site page provides interactive tools to explore temporal trends, spatial patterns, and process-response relationships that drive coastal evolution.

Supporting the Victorian Coastal Monitoring Program

This platform contributes to the objectives of the Victorian Coastal Monitoring Program (VCMP), which was established in 2017 to:

  • Increase understanding of how coastlines change over time through systematic data collection and analysis
  • Assist coastal management in the present by providing evidence-based insights for decision-making
  • Build coastal resilience for the future by identifying vulnerabilities and informing adaptation strategies

Increasing sea levels and more frequent storm events mean that Victoria's coastline may be subjected to increasing coastal erosion and inundation. Coastal monitoring is crucial to understanding coastal change and provides an evidentiary basis for sound coastal management and adaptation.

A critical goal of the VCMP is to aggregate and integrate data from the local to statewide scale, to aid decision-making at local and regional levels. The VCMP is now a major field monitoring and knowledge management program that provides coastal managers and communities with information on coastal processes and hazards.

Port Fairy Bay Overview

Port Fairy Bay, located on Victoria's southwest coast, represents a critical case study for understanding sandy beach response to climate change and coastal engineering interventions. This sandy embayment has been subjected to seven years (2018-2025) of intensive monitoring using high-resolution Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) surveys combined with continuous offshore wave measurements.

The Challenge

Sandy beaches worldwide face mounting pressure from climate-driven sea level rise and human modifications to the coastline. At Port Fairy, rock armour structures intended to protect infrastructure have created a complex pattern of morphological change. Understanding how these beaches respond to different wave conditions and how protection structures alter natural sediment transport processes is essential for sustainable coastal management.

Monitoring Approach

Our research employs spatially distributed metrics across 54 surveys and 220 transects to quantify sediment behavior and process-response relationships. This comprehensive dataset reveals distinct patterns of vulnerability across the beach profile and provides quantitative thresholds that trigger morphological change.

Key Findings

  • Cross-shore vulnerability gradient: The upper beach (above 0.47 m AHD) experiences persistent erosion strongly linked to extreme wave conditions, while the intertidal zone remains relatively stable.
  • Wave height threshold: A critical wave height of 4.6 m marks the transition from beach building to beach erosion in supratidal areas, with prolonged exposure (>30 hours) producing elevation losses exceeding 0.4 m.
  • Rock armour impacts: Protection structures disrupt natural sediment transport, causing erosion on the downdrift side and flanking erosion up to 200 m from structure edges, while redistributing sediment from the upper to lower beach.
  • Future vulnerability: Under projected sea level rise, the upper beach will increasingly experience direct wave attack, creating a "coastal squeeze" where armoring prevents natural landward migration.
  • Management implications: The findings provide quantitative thresholds for early warning systems and demonstrate the need for adaptive management strategies that consider natural sediment transport processes.

This research offers a transferable vulnerability framework applicable to data-limited coastlines globally, supporting the transition from reactive hard engineering to adaptive, nature-based coastal management under accelerating climate change.

Scientific Studies

In Review

Cross-shore vulnerability gradients and rock armour impacts on sandy beach evolution

Nuyts, S., Kennedy, D.M., Ierodiaconou, D., et al.

Coastal Engineering (2025) - In Review

This study uses seven years of high-resolution UAV surveys and offshore wave monitoring to assess morphodynamic changes and the influence of rock armour structures on Port Fairy Bay. Results reveal distinct cross-shore vulnerability gradients and quantify wave height thresholds that trigger morphological change. The research demonstrates how hard protection structures disrupt natural sediment transport patterns and amplify future coastal vulnerability under sea level rise.

Published

Structure-from-motion photogrammetry analysis of historical aerial photography: Determining beach volumetric change over decadal scales

Carvalho, R.C., Kennedy, D.M., Niyazi, Y., Leach, C., Konlechner, T.M., Ierodiaconou, D.

Earth Surface Processes and Landforms, 45, 2540-2555 (2020)

Demonstrates the application of historical aerial photography and Structure-from-Motion techniques to quantify long-term beach volumetric changes in the Port Fairy region, providing decadal-scale context for contemporary UAV monitoring.

Published

Predicting compartment-scale climate change impacts related to southern ocean wave forcing: Port Fairy, Victoria, Australia

Leach, C., Kennedy, D.M., Carvalho, R.C., Ierodiaconou, D.

Journal of Marine Science and Engineering (2020)

Investigates the relationship between Southern Ocean wave climate and coastal change at Port Fairy, providing insights into how climate-driven changes in wave forcing will impact future coastal evolution.
